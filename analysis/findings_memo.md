# Missouri Vehicle Stops: The Hispanic Surge
## Story Outline — April 2026

---

## I. Opening

Between 2014 and 2024, Missouri police dialed back traffic enforcement for nearly everyone — by about a quarter, per capita. White per-capita stop rate fell 24%. Black per-capita stop rate fell 22%. Hispanic per-capita stop rate: essentially flat (+2%). Hispanic drivers are the one group who did not share in the statewide enforcement pullback.

In raw numbers, the divergence is stark. Stops of white drivers fell 25%. Stops of Black drivers fell 28%. Stops of Hispanic drivers rose 52%. Missouri's Hispanic population grew 49% over the same period, so the raw count increase is roughly matched by population growth — but the *comparison* to white and Black per-capita rates, which each dropped by about a quarter, is the real finding.

This isn't a COVID rebound story. Hispanic stops in 2019 — before the pandemic — were already at 35,526, a historic high. By 2024 they'd reached 46,655, the highest count on record. Every other group's raw stop count is sharply lower today than a decade ago. Hispanic is the exception, and the divergence widened after 2021.

[CHART: Indexed stops by race, 2014=100. Three lines. Two sloping down. One climbing above 150 after 2021.]

Meanwhile, more than 60 Missouri law enforcement agencies have signed agreements with ICE that let officers ask about immigration status during routine traffic stops. The federal government is offering those agencies cars, equipment, and cash bonuses for officers who locate undocumented people. But the Hispanic stop acceleration started years before those agreements were signed — raising a question the data alone can't answer: was the enforcement culture already shifting before the formal agreements made it policy?

## II. What the data shows

### The per-capita divergence

Missouri's Hispanic population grew 48.6% between 2014 and 2024, from 233,325 to 346,700 (American Community Survey). Stops of Hispanic drivers grew 51.6% — just barely faster than the population itself. On a per-capita basis, Hispanic stops are essentially **flat**: 13.2 stops per 100 Hispanic residents in 2014, 13.5 in 2024 (+2%).

White population barely moved (−2%) and white stops fell 25%, so the per-capita white stop rate dropped from 27.2 to 20.7 — a 24% reduction. Black population fell 8% and Black stops fell 28%, so the per-capita Black stop rate dropped from 43.2 to 33.6 — a 22% reduction.

The comparison is the finding:

| | Per-capita stop rate 2014 | Per-capita stop rate 2024 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| White | 27.2 | 20.7 | **−24%** |
| Black | 43.2 | 33.6 | **−22%** |
| Hispanic | 13.2 | 13.5 | **+2%** |

Missouri cut its per-capita traffic enforcement by nearly a quarter across the decade. Hispanic drivers got none of that reduction. Note also that Black drivers are still the most over-stopped group in Missouri in per-capita terms — 33.6 per 100, more than double the Hispanic rate (13.5) and 1.6× the white rate (20.7). This story is not a claim that Hispanic drivers are the most-policed population. It's a claim about *direction of change*.

[CHART: Per-capita stops per 100 residents, by race, 2014–2024.]

The raw-count acceleration shows up after 2021. Year-over-year, Hispanic stops jumped double-digit percentages in 2022, 2023, and 2024 — while white and Black counts continued drifting downward. On a per-capita basis the Hispanic line ticks up modestly over the same years; the statewide pullback in enforcement for white and Black drivers did not extend to Hispanic drivers even as the acceleration kicked in.

### Per-stop harshness declined — for everyone

Here's the part of this story that changed when we extended the data back to 2014. Earlier drafts framed the Hispanic arrest rate as *climbing* between 2020 and 2024. With the longer window, that framing collapses.

The Hispanic per-stop arrest rate actually *declined* over 11 years, from 8.19% in 2014 to 6.17% in 2024 — about a 25% drop. That's almost exactly the same percentage drop that white (−18%) and Black (−26%) drivers saw. Search rates, investigative stop rates, and equipment stop rates also fell for every race. None of these per-stop rate changes are uniquely Hispanic.

Two things make Hispanic drivers the exception anyway:

1. **The disparity never closed.** Hispanic drivers were still arrested at roughly 1.8 times the white rate in 2024 (6.17% vs. 3.38%). That ratio is essentially unchanged from 2014. It isn't a gap that's opening — it's a gap that never closed.

2. **The absolute count rose.** White arrests fell from roughly 54,500 to 33,300 — a loss of 21,000. Black arrests fell from 24,000 to 12,700 — a loss of 11,000. Hispanic arrests rose from 2,520 to 2,880 — a gain of about 360. Hispanic is the only race whose raw arrest count went up, because volume grew faster than the per-stop rate softened.

[METHODOLOGICAL NOTE — not in the story, for editors: Missouri law requires a search incident to every arrest, so search rate and arrest rate move together mechanically. Don't present search rate as an independent trend. The arrest disparity plus the volume surge is the clean finding.]

### The composition shift: license stops doubled for Hispanic drivers

Not all traffic stops are created equal. Moving violations leave little room for discretion. License stops — pulling a driver over for a missing, expired, or invalid license — are the category most likely to surface immigration status. A driver without a license can't prove who they are, and that opens the door to everything that happens next.

Here the comparative picture is unusual. The *share* of stops that are license stops rose for every race across the decade: white drivers from 22.99% to 32.85%, Black drivers from 30.41% to 40.73%, Hispanic drivers from 19.65% to 25.89%. As a statewide composition shift, license stops climbed for everyone. White drivers actually saw the biggest proportional jump (+43%).

But because Hispanic stop volume grew 52% while white and Black volumes fell, the composition shift lands on very different absolute populations:

| | White | Black | Hispanic |
|---|---|---|---|
| License stops 2014 | 302,797 | 92,558 | 6,048 |
| License stops 2024 | 323,283 | 88,746 | 12,080 |
| Change | +7% | −4% | **+100%** |

White license stops barely moved. Black license stops fell. Hispanic license stops literally doubled. It's the same composition shift, but because Hispanic drivers were the only group getting stopped in rising numbers, the license-stop bucket captured almost all of that volume surge.

[CHART: Absolute license stop counts by race, 2014–2024. Hispanic line climbs; white is flat; Black is slightly declining.]

This is the finding that matters for the ICE pipeline story — not because Hispanic drivers are being stopped for license reasons at a uniquely high per-stop rate (they aren't), but because they're the only population for whom license-stop *exposure* meaningfully grew. Twice as many Hispanic drivers in 2024 had an officer's first question be "Can I see your license?" — the question that ended Victor López Delara's O'Fallon traffic stop in February 2026 with his transfer to ICE custody.

## III. Where it's happening

The surge isn't uniform. It clustered in specific corridors, and the agencies with the worst numbers overlap closely with the agencies now signing immigration enforcement agreements — and the facilities producing the most ICE detainers.

[MAP: Agency-level Hispanic stop growth, 2014–2024. Color by percent change, size by volume.]

### It's not just the Highway Patrol

MSHP is the biggest single agency — about a quarter of all stops. But the statewide surge isn't an MSHP story. MSHP's own Hispanic stop count was flat for most of the decade, hovering around 10,000 a year from 2014 through 2021, and only started climbing in 2022. By 2024 it reached 14,569. The inflection point at MSHP is 2022 — the same year the statewide Hispanic curve turns sharply upward. Something changed that year. We don't yet know what.

MSHP has never submitted written comment to the attorney general explaining any of its data.

## IV. The ICE connection

### The detainer data

ICE detainers issued at Missouri facilities went from roughly 250 in late 2023 to nearly 2,000 in 2025. Street-level custodial arrests — encounters outside a jail — went from zero to over 400, all in 2025.

The same agencies producing the sharpest Hispanic stop increases are also producing the most ICE detainers: Greene County, Jasper County, St. Charles County, Christian County, Taney County.

[CHART or TABLE: ICE detainers by Missouri facility, 2023–2025, matched to VSR agencies.]

[CAVEAT: The detainer data doesn't record how the encounter started. You can't prove a specific traffic stop led to a specific detainer. But the geographic and temporal overlap is striking, and the doubling of Hispanic license stops gives you a clear mechanism.]

### The Creve Coeur example

A documented case from April 2025: a man stopped just after midnight, no moving violation, passed a sobriety test, arrested for driving without a license after telling the officer he didn't have one and was in the country illegally. The officer called ICE. The man was later charged with illegal re-entry. Creve Coeur doesn't have a 287(g) — they have a long-standing policy to contact ICE when encountering suspected undocumented individuals in custody.

Creve Coeur is in the data. Their Hispanic stops rose sharply across the decade.

[NEEDS REPORTING: Request the body cam footage. Are there other documented stop-to-ICE cases in the high-disparity agencies?]

### The incentive structure

Per the First Alert 4 investigation: agencies that sign 287(g) agreements receive vehicles, equipment, and salary reimbursement for trained officers. Individual officers are eligible for "monetary performance awards based on locating undocumented individuals and overall assistance to further ICE's mission."

[NEEDS REPORTING: FOIA the 287(g) MOAs for the specific financial terms. How much per officer? How are "performance awards" calculated?]

### The timeline

The first Missouri 287(g) wasn't signed until March 2025. The Hispanic stop acceleration runs 2022–2024. The agreements didn't cause the trend. But they institutionalized and incentivized an enforcement posture the data suggests was already building.

## V. The silence

### The comment collapse

The state gives agencies a chance to explain their numbers every year. In the early 2020s, roughly 500 agencies responded. In 2024, eighteen did. The agencies with the sharpest Hispanic stop increases are overwhelmingly among the silent.

MSHP — the state's largest agency — has a row in the comment file every year. They have never submitted actual text.

[TABLE: High-disparity agencies cross-referenced with comment history.]

### The AG tried to stop publishing the disparity data

In 2023, the attorney general removed the disparity index — the core measure of whether police are stopping people in proportion to their population — from the annual report. He called it of "limited analytical value." The NAACP sued. They settled in December 2025. The AG agreed to put it back.

### Nobody was watching

Every prior story about the Missouri VSR focused on Black-white disparities. The Hispanic trend was in the same data the whole time, going back 11 years.

## VII. Closing

The data can't prove motive. What it shows is that Missouri dialed back traffic enforcement across the board — except for Hispanic drivers. It shows the volume surge accelerated after 2021. It shows the composition of those stops got funneled into the one category most likely to connect to the immigration enforcement apparatus. It shows the agencies responsible have largely declined to explain it.

This data exists because Missouri passed a law in 2000 specifically to detect racial bias in policing. Twenty-six years later, the state collects the data, publishes it, invites agencies to explain it — and almost nobody does.
